Introduction   DOE Environmental Management (EM) faces a persistent data-to-decision bottleneck.  Data acquired from environmental characterization, monitoring, and site interpretation are often scattered among heterogeneous measurements, legacy reports, and site-specific documents, not ready for AI applications and informed decision-making.  Consequently, high-value historical information, such as environmental characterization, detailed process sampling, remediation, monitoring, and site/facility operations datasets, remains difficult to discover, connect, and reuse in a timely way.  We aim to fill this gap by curating data sets, harmonizing multimodal data preparation and creating intelligent data retrieval and analytics via an AI-driven human-in-the loop knowledge system to accelerate EM decision-making and co-design waste management for the DOE complex with novel geospatial analysis and visualization enabled by AI.  This project will create a containerized, deployable and transformative AI-guided data and knowledge workflow that converts fragmented environmental and operational records into harmonized, verifiable information layers to support sensible decision-making for DOE EM sites.  This AI framework enhances evidence retrieval performance, achieving higher Precision@k while remaining accountable cross-source data synthesis, and more effective AI-enhanced decision support for characterization, monitoring, remediation, and waste management workflows.  We will use the well-established site operation data at ORNL and Y-12 in addition to background environmental data to establish the framework.1,2  We will employ exploratory data analysis (EDA) to discover data trends,3 AI to predict potential anomalies,4 and an AI-guided workflow5 for multimodal data harmonization, analysis, and scenario queries to support informed decision-making for DOE EM sites.  
Project Objectives   The objective is to demonstrate that historical environmental measurements and legacy data and documents can be transformed into a harmonized and retrieval-ready information data structure that supports informed decision workflows and provides a reliable foundation for broader cross-site and cross-media AI deployment.  Phase I will: 1) establish a traceable, AI-guided workflow for pattern and anomaly discovery, organization, and harmonization of EM-relevant historical data and documents, 2) establish an integrated AI-assisted decision making framework that enables retrieval, synthesis, and use of these data assets to support waste management, storage, and transport and provide a foundation for broad deployment within DOE, and 3) create a bidirectional, human-in-the-loop interface that supports expert knowledge capture, user feedback, and workforce-facing delivery of AI-synthesized insights.  The Phase I project will validate the core workflow in a domain with a high technical readiness level (i.e., TRL>5) using air data.  Phase II will extend the capabilities to water- and soil-related datasets and synchronize with the R&D road map implementation in the DOE complex.  The innovative AI platform proposed herein, offering data harmonization, analytics, geospatial visualization, and decision-making, will position ORNL for leadership in implementing the EM AI R&D roadmap.  Utilizing the rich QA/QCed data curated at ORNL and Y-12 and the computational resources at ORNL (Frontier, NERSC HPC) and UTK, we have a strong foundation to succeed in Phase I.  Collaboration with UTK will provide training for the next-generation workforce in Tennessee's booming nuclear industry, including fission and fusion, across TVA, Kairos, and Type One Energy. 
Proposed Research and Methods   The 9-month (M) effort will establish a proof-of-concept AI-guided data and knowledge workflow for the EM environmental data use case.  We will perform three tasks.
Task 1. AI-Guided Data Curation and Harmonization  (M1 – M4). Task 1 directly supports the Data Curation component of the AI R&D Roadmap and establishes the quality benchmarks and monitoring foundations needed for later deployment and improvement. Task 1.1. AI-guided data discovery and organization.  We will identify and organize historical data assets across three source tiers: (1) expert-curated foundational datasets, including NEPA Environmental Impact Statements (EIS) and associated EPA EIS database records, EM cleanup site operation data from ORCC, tabular environmental and operational datasets, and other authoritative historical records; (2) expert-selected literature and knowledge sources, including books, journal articles, and technical reports; and (3) MCP-enabled retrieval and crawl data used to supplement and update the knowledge base. For each source, AI agents will assist with query generation, document retrieval, source classification, duplicate detection, and relevance screening against predefined EM decision questions and priority variables. Because crawl data may be less reliable than expert-curated and operational sources, it will be assigned lower source-confidence scores and routed through provenance review and expert validation before incorporation An AI-guided workflow will be further developed to support source discovery, document screening, and initial relevance assessment based on our recent success in Automating Geospatial Web Dashboards Using Large Language Models and AI Agents for Decision Support.5 The primary outcome will be a prioritized inventory of candidate data assets, including source type, relevance score, confidence level, key variables, spatial/temporal coverage, operational relevance, and downstream use cases.

Task 1.2. Multimodal data harmonization and repository development. Using the prioritized sources identified in Task 1.1, we will construct a harmonized, traceable, and extensible multimodal repository that links structured environmental and operational datasets with unstructured historical and technical records. The initial air-domain repository will build on an existing ORR meteorological dataset containing structured measurements of temperature, pressure, humidity, wind, precipitation, and solar radiation. We will extend this site-based air dataset into an AI-ready multimodal repository by linking structured monitoring data with document-derived context from EIS reports, agency records, technical reports, literature, and MCP-retrieved updates. Structured datasets will be harmonized by variable, unit, location, timestamp, and reporting period, while unstructured documents will be parsed into retrieval-ready segments and annotated with metadata, including source, document type, site/location, date, environmental medium, key variables, and decision context. A common harmonization schema will align records across variables, locations, time windows, document types, and provenance fields. Shared identifiers will connect air-domain measurements with relevant document segments, regulatory records, and technical knowledge. After completing the air-domain repository, we will assess candidate soil and water datasets based on data quality, quantity, metadata completeness, spatial-temporal coverage, and relevance to EM decision questions. Selected soil or water datasets will then be incorporated into the same Task 1 harmonization workflow to support cross-domain validation in Task 2. Task 1.3. Data quality benchmarking, cataloging, and readiness assessment.  To support AI-ready data preparation, we will implement a lightweight data quality layer for data cleaning, validation, deduplication, source-confidence assessment, and schema conformance during ingestion. Quality checks will assess completeness, spatial consistency, temporal coherence, provenance confidence, unit consistency, duplication, and cross-source linkage quality. Machine-readable metadata and cataloging will support discoverability, lineage tracing, retrieval readiness, and future incorporation of additional data from users and partners through common metadata and schema standards. For the air-domain foundation, the prior ORR meteorological dataset already provides a quality-controlled starting point. This project will build on those quality-control procedures and extend them into a broader AI-readiness framework that evaluates both structured datasets and document-derived knowledge sources. For soil and water datasets, readiness assessment will be used as a selection and screening step to determine whether the data are sufficient for harmonization, retrieval, analytics, and cross-domain testing. The overall outcome of Task 1 will be a harmonized, traceable, and knowledge-ready multimodal data repository, beginning with an AI-enhanced air-domain foundation and extending to selected soil and/or water datasets as data quality and availability allow. This repository, together with metadata, quality benchmarks, and readiness indicators, will provide the data-curation foundation for downstream retrieval, synthesis, analytics, deployment, and continuous-improvement workflows under the EM AI R&D roadmap.
Task 2.  AI-Enhanced EM Analytics (M1 – M6). Task 2 will build an analytics workflow for transforming the AI-ready repository developed in Task 1 into adaptive monitoring and early-warning support for EM decision-making. Task 2.1 Air-domain adaptive monitoring and early-warning analytics. Using the AI-ready repository developed in Task 1, we will develop an air-domain analytics workflow that builds on our prior EDA and machine-learning analyses of ORR air-domain data. This analytics workflow will include EDA to characterize temporal trends, spatial patterns, correlations among key air-related variables, and clustering patterns associated with extreme weather conditions. It will also include machine-learning models for predictive analytics, including forecasting selected air-quality indicators, identifying high-risk periods or locations, and evaluating variable importance. The workflow will support adaptive and near-real-time monitoring concepts for early warning of contamination events and critical infrastructure or site-operation failures. Task 2.2 Cross-domain analytics validation using water/soil data. To evaluate whether the air-domain analytics workflow can be rapidly adapted to other EM-relevant environmental media, we will apply the workflow to representative water or soil datasets selected in Task 1. Domain-specific variables, such as contaminant concentration, sampling depth, environmental medium, sampling method, location, and reporting period, will be mapped to the shared schema and analyzed using the same evaluation framework. We will compare results across air and water/soil to identify reusable workflow components, domain-specific adjustments, and modifications needed for Phase II scale-up. This task will test whether the common schema, analytics-oriented feature construction, EDA procedures, and predictive modeling framework can be extended beyond the initial air-domain use case. Validation will assess whether the workflow can align records across locations and time periods, identify patterns and trends, support initial predictive analysis, and generate traceable outputs for EM monitoring and decision support in additional environmental media. The outcome will be a validation summary documenting reusable components, domain-specific requirements, model-validation results, and recommendations for rapid deployment across additional EM sites and data domains in Phase II.

The overall outcome of Task 2 will be an AI-enhanced environmental monitoring and analytics workflow that transforms the AI-ready repository from Task 1 into interpretable and traceable support for EM decision-making. The workflow will support EM cleanup site operations, including environmental monitoring, remediation, nuclear fuel and waste management, waste disposal, storage, transport, and related mission activities. Task 3.  Bidirectional, Generational Human-in-the-Loop Decision-making Framework  (M3 – M9).  
We will develop a bidirectional user interface that packages Task 2 decision-support capabilities into practical tools for domain experts, site managers, and field personnel. It supports: 1) expert-to-system knowledge capture through conversational and visual tools, and 2) system-to-user delivery of AI-synthesized insights via adaptive dashboards and natural-language outputs tailored to the user's expertise level. The tool is Docker containerized for rapid deployment at DOE EM sites. The interface will also support autonomous controls and robots in hazardous environments by providing decision-support API consumable by automated systems.

Task 3.1 Bidirectional human-in-the-loop system:

Knowledge Integration and Context: This layer unifies foundational, knowledge, and crawling data with environmental outputs into a semantic backbone for context-aware6 mRAG. integrating multimodal data across three layers and Task 2 modeling into a scenario knowledge graph7. Transparent data lineage is maintained through CoT reconstruction underlying each recommendation and assessment8. To ensure factual consistency in agent outputs6, the knowledge integration layer dynamically constructs scenario knowledge graphs from user queries, iteratively refined via HIL. Requests are embedded using domain-adapted sentence transformers9 and re-indexed in a vector store with hybrid sparse-dense retrieval10, linking each passage to provenance metadata (source, version, timestamp). This grounding anchors decision-support outputs in QA/QC-verified site data, improving evidence retrieval and mixed image-text metrics11 while supporting transparent cross-source synthesis. The system maintains time-stamped snapshots of the knowledge graph12 and monitors data streams for distributional shifts relative to baseline embeddings13. When drift is detected, it triggers targeted re-indexing and agent recalibration, flagging outlier assessments for human review14. Agent autonomy is dynamically adjusted based on task novelty, data sensitivity, and model uncertainty 15 following the Sheridan–Verplank framework, which automates routine tasks while preserving expert oversight for high-uncertainty decisions16 The knowledge layer functions as an integrated knowledge management system, ensuring data accessibility across EM sites and supporting operating & administrative procedures through contextualized retrieval. Supervisor, Task Specifier, and Orchestration: A hierarchical supervisor agent will coordinate specialized agents and workflows, requiering HIL input throughout all decision-making cycles within the pipeline17. This central agentic ecosystem in which the supervisor constrains the chain of thought based on HIL enables adaptive reasoning18,19. A planning agent processes incoming queries by formalizing the objective, classifying intent (exploratory analysis, intervention evaluation, anomaly investigation, or compliance assessment), and selecting the appropriate tools for the workflow domain (characterization, monitoring, remediation, or waste management 20,21. The orchestration layer also supports integrated performance and risk assessment and risk-informed decision analyses by routing queries through specialized assessment agents This hierarchical multi-agent framework will be implemented using AutoGen-style multi-agent conversation patterns22 enabling modular agent specialization while maintaining coherent orchestration across the decision-support pipeline.
 Multi-agent Conversation and Conflict Resolution: Specialized agents (data harmonization, trend analysis, geospatial visualization, regulatory compliance) operate semi-autonomously via structured message protocols23. When agents produce conflicting assessments, a conflict resolution agent applies weighted evidence aggregation using domain-specific reliability context24, to produce a consolidated result that documents the disagreement, resolution rationale, and residual uncertainty. Cases with unresolved conflict are escalated for human-in-the-loop review rather than autonomous resolution25. These tasks support system-to-user delivery.

3.2. Interactive bidirectional interface layer:

Explainable AI Visualizations and Uncertainty Communication: Effective HIL decision-making requires uncertainty communicated through visual encodings. Generated UIs will include confidence-mapped geospatial overlays of EM site data, temporal monitoring trend projections, and interactive sensitivity diagrams highlighting the most influential assessment parameters. Uncertainty will be stratified into aleatory and epistemic components26. Visualizations will support environmental liability and financial forecasts, enabling stakeholders to evaluate cleanup cost trajectories against the estimated $540 billion liability. Sketch-to-interface generation pipeline: A sketch-to-code pipeline27 converts hand-drawn SVG mockups into React dashboards by: (1) interpreting sketches with a vision-language model; (2) generating modular components (Leaflet, Recharts, Tailwind); and (3) validating and repairing code via agents28. A "what-if" interface29 enables iterative modification of monitoring, remediation, and waste management settings, triggering HIL re-evaluation with each change including optimization of waste treatment processes, remedial systems, and waste disposition parameters,. Human feedback refines outputs while the UI ensures compliance, auditability, and flags cases requiring human judgment30. DOE EM decision-making involves multiple stakeholders:site managers, environmental scientists, compliance officers, and waste management specialists. The interface layer provides collaborative annotation capabilities21 Users can attach structured comments, alternative interpretations, or change requests to specific assessments, map regions, or scenario parameters. Each annotation is versioned and linked to the corresponding data and model states through a W3C PROV-compliant provenance ontology31,  producing a complete decision audit trail that meets regulatory documentation requirements. 

Task 3.3 Feedback-Driven Optimization 
 
The system accumulates validated decisions and expert corrections for continual improvement4. via: 1) supervised fine-tuning on initial-to-corrected output pairs, and 2) preference optimization using Direct Preference Optimization32 from pairwise rankings. For multi-step behaviors tool selection, agent routing, HIL escalation, and multimodal query refinement RLHF33 optimizes the orchestration policy end-to-end using aggregated human approval signals across the full characterization, monitoring, remediation, and waste management workflow. Updates are staged in a sandbox and evaluated against EM benchmarks before deployment to prevent catastrophic forgetting or behavioral drift34 with version-controlled checkpoints ensuring reproducibility and regulatory auditability. The optimization loop extends to the mRAG retrieval layer via implicit signals and correction annotations, enabling continuous improvement in accuracy, contextual relevance, and alignment with expert judgment over time.
